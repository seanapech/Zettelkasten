# How to create a new note

Hello, Sean. This note was specifically created for the event that you forget how to write, format, and organize a note. While these may sound like obvious things at first, it's important that you abide by the very simple rules that you have designated for yourself, that way your notes and its presiding organizational paradigm wield some level of continuity.

The first point to address is actually creating the new note file. To do this, use the hotkey: ``CTRL + O.

When you use this hotkey, you can simply type the name of the note you intend to write and press ``Enter``. The usefulness of this hotkey versus ``CTRL + N`` can be demonstrated by the fact that you can also see what other notes you have already made while typing out the name of your new note; You can avoid duplication of efforts.

Once you've created your note, press ``CTRL + Q`` to select a template. There are two templates that I have created, which are a 'Full Note' and a 'Source Note'. These note templates captures four important components of your future notes: Name of the note, status of development, tags, and references/source material.

You will use the status hashtags #u-developed, #s-developed, and #f-developed, which stand for underdeveloped, somewhat-developed, and fully-developed respective. This is to remind yourself which notes need more attending to, and which ones are more fleshed-out. Additionally, #source and #permanent will be used specifically for 'source' and 'permanent' notes.

Finally, the most important part of your note-taking journey will be outlined here; You must organize your notes in terms of rough notes, referential notes, and permanent notes, all with the use of **tagging**.

**Rough notes**, or fleeting notes should only contain thought, ideas, or random bits of information that you felt like taking note of at that time; These are rough, unadulterated, but non-comprehensive notes. They can be used, perhaps, as reminders of thing you need to do, things you want to read about, or simple journaling? It's up to you.

**Source material**, or referential notes, should specifically contain information about the source (e.g. book, website w/ link, person, etc.) and your own interpretation of the source material; Use your own words, don't fall victim to **passive learning**.

**Permanent notes** are essentially the pinnacle of a particular topic you've learned about. Permanent notes should be independent, holistic interpretations or mini-essays about a topic of interest that you learned about through writing referential notes. Be sure to use hyperlinking in the reference section to connect these permanent notes back to the source material you used to write the note, and select relevant tags as well.

Finally, **tags** are very simple. Add tags to a note, not based on topics you believe the note is related to in a broader sense, but based on how you think it will be used in the future; *Make sure the tags serve **you**.* When there are enough notes associated with a single tag, like 6-8+ for example--or really when the amount of notes exceeds how far you can see in the empty tag page with backlinking--make sure to start indexing those tags. In other words, start organizing the hyperlinks to those notes and organize them using headers, or whatever works for you.

In conclusion, try to keep your notes simple and readable. Ideally, you should keep notes shorter than the space needed before you have to scroll down to read the rest, and also think about employing regular breaks/spaces between sentences where natural breaks occur; It's much easier to read smaller bits rather than one large, ugly blob of text. Clearly I did not abide by some of these rules in this note, but I will try to do better as I move forward!
